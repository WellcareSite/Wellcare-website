## Start Planning Now — Not in August

The best time to prepare for the school year is **weeks before it starts**. For children with IEPs, sensory needs, or behavioral challenges, early preparation can make the difference between a smooth transition and a rough September.

## Step 1: Review Your Child's IEP or 504 Plan

If your child has an IEP or 504 plan, request a copy and review it:

- Are the goals still relevant?
- Were last year's accommodations effective?
- Do you need to request changes or additions?
- Has your child made progress that changes their needs?

**Pro tip:** Request an IEP meeting *before* school starts if you need to make changes. You have the right to request a meeting at any time.

## Step 2: Communicate with the New Teacher

Don't assume information transfers smoothly between school years. Proactively reach out to your child's new teacher with:

- A brief summary of your child's strengths and challenges
- What strategies work best (and what doesn't)
- Sensory needs or triggers
- Communication preferences (daily notes, email, app)
- Your contact information and availability

Consider creating a **one-page student snapshot** — a simple document that gives the teacher everything they need to know at a glance.

## Step 3: Practice the New Routine

For children who struggle with transitions, **practice the new routine before school starts**:

- Drive to the school together and walk the route
- Practice the morning routine (wake up, get dressed, eat, leave)
- Visit the classroom if the school allows it
- Read social stories about starting school
- Use a visual schedule showing the daily routine

### Visual Schedule Tips:
- Use pictures or icons your child recognizes
- Include transitions ("first breakfast, then shoes, then car")
- Post it where your child can see it independently
- Practice with it for at least a week before school starts

## Step 4: Address Sensory Needs

School environments can be overwhelming. Talk with your OT about:

- **Noise:** Cafeteria, assemblies, fire drills — does your child need noise-reducing headphones?
- **Seating:** Wobble cushions, resistance bands on chair legs, or preferential seating
- **Sensory breaks:** Can your child access a calm corner or movement break?
- **Clothing:** Tags, seams, and textures — sort out uniforms or school clothes now
- **Cafeteria:** Food textures, smells, social dynamics during lunch

## Step 5: Build Social Skills Before Day One

If your child struggles socially, summer is the time to practice:

- Arrange playdates with future classmates
- Practice greetings, turn-taking, and conversation starters
- Role-play common school scenarios (raising hand, asking for help, joining a group)
- Work with your speech or ABA therapist on specific social goals

## Step 6: Prepare for the Emotional Side

Starting a new school year brings big feelings — for you and your child:

### For your child:
- Validate their feelings: "It's okay to feel nervous about new things"
- Focus on what they're excited about
- Create a "worry box" — write down worries and put them in a box to "hold" them
- Establish a goodbye routine that's quick and consistent

### For you:
- Connect with other parents of children with special needs
- Write down your concerns and questions for the teacher
- Remember: you are your child's best advocate
- It's okay to feel anxious too

## How Therapy Supports School Success

Your child's therapy team can help with back-to-school preparation:

| Therapy | How It Helps |
|---------|-------------|
| **Speech** | Social communication scripts, vocabulary for school contexts, self-advocacy phrases |
| **OT** | Sensory strategies for the classroom, handwriting prep, self-regulation tools |
| **PT** | Navigating the school environment, playground skills, endurance for long days |
| **ABA** | Transition strategies, behavioral support plans, social skills practice |

**Ask your therapist** to share relevant strategies with your child's teacher — with your permission, we can provide school-friendly summaries.

## The First Week Survival Guide

1. **Keep afternoons low-key** — school is exhausting, especially at first
2. **Expect some regression** — behaviors may increase as your child adjusts
3. **Check in daily** — not just "how was school?" but specific questions
4. **Stay connected with the teacher** — early communication prevents problems
5. **Be patient** — adjustment takes 2–4 weeks for most children
