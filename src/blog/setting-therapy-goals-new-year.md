## Reflecting on the Year

Before looking forward, take a moment to look back. Your child has likely made more progress than you realize — because when you see them every day, growth can feel invisible.

### Questions to Ask Yourself:
- What could my child do in January that they couldn't do the year before?
- What moments surprised me?
- What strategies worked well at home?
- What felt like the biggest challenges?
- What am I most proud of?

### Questions to Ask Your Therapist:
- "What progress have you seen this year?"
- "Where are the biggest areas of growth?"
- "What should we focus on next?"
- "Are there skills that are almost there but need a push?"
- "What can we do differently at home?"

## How to Set Meaningful Therapy Goals

Good therapy goals follow the **SMART** framework:

| Element | What It Means | Example |
|---------|--------------|---------|
| **Specific** | Clear, defined skill | "Use 3-word sentences" (not "talk more") |
| **Measurable** | You can track progress | "In 4 out of 5 opportunities" |
| **Achievable** | Challenging but realistic | Based on current ability level |
| **Relevant** | Matters to daily life | Impacts home, school, or community participation |
| **Time-bound** | Has a target date | "By June 2027" |

### Examples of Strong Goals:

**Speech:** "By June 2027, Maya will use 3–4 word sentences to request items and activities in 80% of opportunities across therapy, home, and school settings."

**OT:** "By June 2027, James will independently complete a 5-step morning routine (brush teeth, get dressed, eat breakfast, pack backpack, put on shoes) using a visual schedule with no more than 1 verbal prompt."

**PT:** "By June 2027, Sofia will climb playground stairs using alternating feet and navigate uneven terrain (grass, gravel) without losing balance in 4 out of 5 attempts."

**ABA:** "By June 2027, Liam will use a functional communication strategy (words, signs, or AAC) to express frustration instead of hitting in 80% of observed instances across home and therapy settings."

## Goals Beyond Therapy

Some of the most important goals happen outside the clinic:

### Family Goals
- "We will practice therapy strategies for 10 minutes during our daily routine"
- "We will attend one community outing per month that challenges our child's skills"
- "We will establish a consistent bedtime routine by February"

### Advocacy Goals
- "We will request an IEP meeting by February to update goals"
- "We will research one new community resource per month"
- "We will connect with two other special needs families this year"

### Self-Care Goals (for parents)
- "I will schedule one activity for myself each week"
- "I will join a parent support group by March"
- "I will ask for help when I need it"

## Working with Your Therapy Team

The best goals are **collaborative** — created by you and your therapy team together. Here's how to make the most of goal-setting conversations:

1. **Come prepared** with your observations and priorities
2. **Be honest** about what's realistic at home
3. **Prioritize ruthlessly** — 2–3 meaningful goals beat 10 vague ones
4. **Ask for home strategies** that fit your actual daily life
5. **Schedule check-ins** — don't wait for the annual review to discuss progress

## Celebrate Progress, Not Perfection

Some years bring leaps. Some years bring slow, steady growth that's harder to see. Both are valuable.

Your child doesn't need to hit every goal on schedule to be making meaningful progress. Development isn't linear — there are bursts, plateaus, and sometimes regressions that precede big jumps.

What matters most is that your child is **supported, loved, and given the opportunity to grow at their own pace.**
