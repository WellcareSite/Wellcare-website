## The Whole Family Is Affected

When your child receives a diagnosis or starts therapy, the focus naturally shifts to that child. Appointments, strategies, accommodations, research — it takes enormous energy. And in the process, siblings sometimes get less attention than they need.

This isn't a failure of parenting. It's the reality of having a child with additional needs. But acknowledging it is the first step toward supporting every child in your family.

## What Siblings May Experience

### Positive Emotions
- **Pride** in their sibling's accomplishments
- **Empathy** and compassion beyond their years
- **Resilience** and adaptability
- **Maturity** and emotional intelligence

### Challenging Emotions
- **Jealousy** — "Why does she get more attention?"
- **Guilt** — "I shouldn't be mad, they can't help it"
- **Embarrassment** — especially in public or with peers
- **Worry** — about their sibling's future, about their own health
- **Resentment** — when family plans change because of their sibling's needs
- **Loneliness** — feeling like the "invisible" child

**All of these feelings are normal and valid.** The goal isn't to prevent them — it's to create space for siblings to feel, express, and process them.

## Signs a Sibling May Be Struggling

- Acting out or regressing in behavior
- Becoming overly "perfect" or self-sufficient (parentified child)
- Withdrawing from family activities
- Changes in school performance
- Physical complaints (headaches, stomachaches)
- Expressing resentment directly: "You only care about [sibling]"
- Taking on a caretaker role with their sibling

## Strategies for Supporting Siblings

### 1. One-on-One Time
Schedule **regular, predictable** individual time with each child. It doesn't have to be elaborate:
- A weekly "date" (ice cream run, walk, library trip)
- 15 minutes of undivided attention at bedtime
- A shared hobby that's just yours and theirs

The key word is **predictable**. When siblings know their time is coming, they can wait more patiently.

### 2. Validate Their Feelings
When a sibling says, "It's not fair," resist the urge to defend or explain. Instead:
- "You're right — it doesn't feel fair sometimes"
- "It's okay to feel frustrated about that"
- "Your feelings matter to me"

Then problem-solve together: "What could we do that would feel better?"

### 3. Explain — Age-Appropriately
Children fill in gaps with imagination, which is often worse than reality. Give siblings age-appropriate information about their sibling's diagnosis or therapy:
- **Ages 3–5:** "Your brother's brain works differently. He goes to therapy to practice things that are hard for him, like talking."
- **Ages 6–9:** "Your sister has autism. That means she sees the world differently. Therapy helps her learn skills that don't come as naturally to her."
- **Ages 10+:** Honest, detailed conversations. Answer their questions. Let them read about the diagnosis if they want.

### 4. Include Them
When appropriate, involve siblings in therapy goals:
- They can be **play partners** during home practice
- Older siblings can learn communication strategies (especially for AAC)
- Let them attend a therapy session occasionally so they understand what happens
- Celebrate the whole family's role in progress

### 5. Watch for Parentification
Some siblings naturally take on a caretaker role. While some responsibility is healthy, watch for signs that it's become a burden:
- Always watching or managing their sibling's behavior
- Sacrificing their own activities for their sibling's needs
- Feeling responsible for their sibling's safety or happiness
- Difficulty being a "regular kid" with peers

It's okay to say: "You don't have to be in charge. That's my job."

### 6. Connect Them with Peers
Sibling support groups and workshops (in-person or online) help children realize they're not alone. Organizations like the **Sibling Support Project** (siblingsupport.org) offer resources specifically for brothers and sisters of children with special needs.

## When to Seek Support for Siblings

Consider connecting a sibling with a counselor or therapist if:
- Emotional or behavioral changes persist beyond a few weeks
- They express ongoing sadness, anger, or hopelessness
- School performance drops significantly
- They become socially isolated
- Physical complaints increase without medical cause
